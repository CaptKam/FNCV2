1. Product Vision
The Soul of the Product
Fork & Compass is a premium culinary travel app built on a single, powerful premise: you can travel the world from your kitchen. Pick a country. Plan a dinner. Shop the ingredients. Cook the meal. Feel like you traveled. The app exists at the intersection of food, culture, and the ritual of gathering around a table.
This is not a recipe database. It is not a meal kit replacement. Fork & Compass is a cultural experience engine that happens to involve cooking. Every interaction should feel like opening a beautifully edited travel magazine and stepping inside.
“Pick a country, cook a dinner, feel like you traveled.”
Origin Story
Born during the COVID era, when friends would pick a country and build an entire evening around its cuisine, drinks, and music to simulate the travel they couldn’t do. That dinner party energy—the planning, the anticipation, the shared experience—is the emotional core of everything Fork & Compass builds.
Who This Is For
Serious home cooks who see cooking as exploration, not obligation. People who already cook well and want to go deeper into world cuisine. The user who spends Sunday afternoon planning the week’s meals, who browses recipes the way others browse travel blogs, and who treats a dinner party as an event worth orchestrating.
Competitive Positioning
Fork & Compass owns culturally authentic world cuisine for serious home cooks. The moat is cultural depth combined with the travel premise. NYT Cooking skews American and European. SideChef pivoted to grocery commerce. HelloFresh validated that people want to cook international food at home but commoditized the experience. No one has built a premium, editorial-quality platform that treats each country’s cuisine as a world worth exploring.

2. Design Philosophy
Every pixel in Fork & Compass is governed by three interlocking design frameworks: Steve Jobs’ functional aesthetics, Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines (including iOS 26 Liquid Glass), and the editorial sensibility of luxury print magazines. These are not decorative references. They are structural foundations that dictate every decision from navigation architecture to button placement to the weight of a shadow.
2.1 — Design Is How It Works
“Most people make the mistake of thinking design is what it looks like. Design is how it works.”  — Steve Jobs
This is the governing law of Fork & Compass. Beautiful food photography means nothing if the user cannot figure out how to plan their week. A glassmorphic tab bar is worthless if it obscures the content it frames. Every aesthetic choice must serve a functional purpose. If a visual element does not help the user accomplish their goal, it is removed.
The implication is absolute: the design team and engineering team are not separate functions operating in sequence. They are a unified discipline. The interface is not a skin applied over logic. The interface is the logic, made visible.
2.2 — The Subtraction Audit
“Simple can be harder than complex. You have to work hard to get your thinking clean to make it simple.”  — Steve Jobs
Fork & Compass applies a rigorous subtraction audit to every screen, every feature, and every interaction:

Audit Test
Question
If It Fails
Mission-Critical

Remove or deeply subordinate the element
Grandma Test
Could a non-technical user complete this without explanation?
Redesign until no instruction is needed

The backward-planning timeline engine is the perfect example. Users don’t want to manually calculate when to start each dish. They want dinner ready at 7:00 PM. The system absorbs all of that complexity—prep times, concurrent dishes, resting periods—and presents one simple output: “Start at 4:25 PM.” The user never sees the algorithm. They see a result that makes their evening effortless.

2.4 — iOS 26 Liquid Glass and Glassmorphic Identity
With iOS 26, Apple introduced Liquid Glass—a translucent, dynamic material that reflects and refracts surrounding content in real time. This is the most significant visual redesign since iOS 7. Fork & Compass embraces Liquid Glass as its native visual identity, not as decoration but as structural design language.
Liquid Glass uses real-time lensing (not simple blur), specular highlights that respond to device motion, adaptive shadows that change based on content context, and content-aware color that samples surrounding imagery. The effect is a UI that feels alive, dimensional, and physically present.

Liquid Glass Implementation Rules
Glass effects apply exclusively to the navigation layer: tab bar, toolbars, sheet headers, floating action overlays. Never applied to content itself.
Three variants: Regular (medium transparency, general use), Clear (high transparency for media-rich hero backgrounds), Identity (no glass effect, for screens where clarity trumps depth).
Food photography, recipe text, and editorial content remain crisp beneath glass surfaces. Content legibility is never sacrificed for aesthetic effect.
Specular highlights respond to device motion via gyroscope data, creating natural depth without demanding attention.
Glass elements use content-aware color: the tab bar subtly shifts warmth over a terracotta hero image, cools over a blue-toned Japanese garden.
On devices below iOS 26, the system degrades gracefully to BlurView frosted glass with identical layout behavior—no feature loss, only reduced visual richness.

2.5 — The Back of the Cabinet
“A true craftsman does not use inferior plywood for the back of a cabinet, even though no one will see it.”  — Paul Jobs, as told by Steve Jobs
The codebase of Fork & Compass is the back of the cabinet. The API server, the Drizzle ORM schema, the timeline engine algorithm, the grocery list category detection—none of this is visible to the user. All of it must be crafted with the same care as the interface. No perpetual beta. No “ship it and fix it later.” No technical debt accepted as inevitable.
This means: code is self-documenting, modular, and rigorously typed with TypeScript 5.9. Internal tooling (the admin dashboard) receives the same design attention as the consumer app. The CLAUDE.md file ensures that even AI-assisted code generation adheres to the design system’s rules.
2.6 — Human-Centric Communication
Fork & Compass speaks like a knowledgeable friend who happens to have traveled the world, not like a software application. Every piece of copy—error messages, button labels, onboarding flows, recipe instructions—is written in warm, active, descriptive language.
Adaptive cooking language across three skill tiers: First Steps (🌱), Home Cook (🍳), Chef’s Table (👨‍🍳). Same ingredients, different instruction voice.
Error states never blame the user. “Something went wrong” becomes “We couldn’t load that recipe. Let’s try again.”
Cultural context is woven into recipes—not as academic footnotes, but as the kind of story a friend tells while you cook together.

Dark Mode Rules
Dark mode is mandatory, not polish. Apple reviewers check it. Broken dark mode is a top cause of App Store rejection.
Never use pure black (#000000) as a background. Fork & Compass dark surface is #121110—a warm near-black with subtle brown undertone that matches the terracotta identity.
Accent colors (terracotta primary) shift to higher brightness and saturation in dark mode (#B85A1A) to maintain visual punch against dark backgrounds.
Text hierarchy uses the onSurface/onSurfaceVariant tokens, which automatically adapt. Never hardcode white text.
Glass surfaces in dark mode reduce their opacity slightly (0.65 vs 0.72) to maintain the sense of depth without washing out underlying content.

Typography Rules
All text supports Dynamic Type. Every text element scales with the user’s accessibility preference. Minimum readable text is 11pt absolute floor; minimum practical text is 13pt (the ‘small’ token).
Hierarchy is maintained through weight and size contrast, never color alone. A visually impaired user must distinguish headline from body without seeing color.
Headlines use Noto Serif for editorial warmth. Body uses Inter for screen-optimized legibility. Never mix these roles.
On dark backgrounds, bump font weight up one level: Regular becomes Medium, Medium becomes SemiBold. This compensates for halation (light text on dark appearing thinner).
Letter spacing tightens at display sizes (-0.5pt) and loosens at small sizes (+0.5pt), matching the San Francisco optical behavior.
NotoSerif_500Medium must be loaded as a font variant. It is required for the ‘title’ token (recipe names, pull quotes).

4. App Architecture and Navigation
4.1 — Tab Structure
Fork & Compass uses a five-tab architecture following Apple HIG conventions. The tab bar is persistent, always visible at the bottom, and never hidden during navigation (exception: full-screen cook mode). On iOS 26+, the tab bar uses NativeTabs with Liquid Glass Regular variant. On older iOS, Android, and web, it uses BlurView frosted glass with identical layout behavior.

Profile/Settings is accessed via the avatar icon in the navigation bar header, not the tab bar. This follows the HIG principle that screen-specific actions never belong in persistent navigation.
4.2 — Navigation Anti-Patterns We Reject
No hamburger menus. Apple App Store reviewers flag this. All primary navigation lives in the tab bar.
No deep modal stacking. Modals are for focused tasks—composing a dinner invite, confirming a delete—not for general navigation.
No hidden gesture-only actions. Every swipe action has a visible button equivalent for accessibility.
No tab bar icons that change meaning by context. A tab is a fixed destination, always.
No StatusBar style hardcoding without theme awareness. Every StatusBar component must respond to the current color scheme.
4.3 — Screen Flow Architecture
Welcome Gate → Onboarding (Country Picker) → Tab Shell. From the tab shell, the user drills into:
Country Detail → Regional Sub-screens → Experience Details
Recipe Detail → Cook Mode (full-screen modal with Liquid Glass Clear toolbar)
Plan → Dinner Party Setup → Guest Management → RSVP
Grocery → In-Store Mode / Delivery Integration
Cook → Multi-Course Timeline Coordinator → Dinner Finish


5. Feature Specifications
5.1 — Discover
The Discover tab is the front door. It must be visually stunning—food photography first, minimal editorial text, maximum visual impact. The user’s eye should be captured by gorgeous imagery before they read a single word.
Hero Carousel
Full-bleed 560px hero images, one per country, horizontal paginated scroll.
Gradient overlay from bottom (overlay token) for text legibility. Country name in displayMedium, flag emoji, editorial blurb in body, “Let’s Go” CTA in titleMedium.
Inline bookmark button in glassmorphic circle (Regular variant, 12px blur) beside the country name.
Terracotta pill pagination dot for active; outlineVariant circle for inactive. 8pt gap between dots.
Country Rings
Horizontally scrollable destination rings below the hero. 94×94 border ring with 88×88 clipped image circle and flag badge.
Active country gets terracotta ring (2pt border, primary token). Inactive rings use outlineVariant.
Tapping syncs the hero carousel with spring animation (280ms, damping: 14).
Below-the-Fold Content
Driven by the active country: tasting menu course tags (horizontal scroll chips), featured recipe journal cards with difficulty badges (horizontal scroll, Radius.md corners), Curator’s Method 3-step section, testimonial pull quote (border-left: primary token, 4pt), and editorial CTA. Pictures-first, editorial-second. Visual density should feel like a food magazine spread, not a settings panel.
5.2 — Search
Apple requires a dedicated search surface with iOS 26. Fork & Compass uses this as a focused discovery tool: search bar with 48pt height (MIN_TAP_TARGET), diet filter chips (sm radius, secondaryContainer fill), cuisine category grid, recent searches, and results spanning both countries and recipes. Minimum row height 56pt. Every result shows a thumbnail, title, and country flag for instant recognition.
5.3 — Plan (The Core Innovation)
Plan is the most strategically important tab. It is the Sunday planning ritual that creates the weekly habit anchor. This is where the user goes from browsing to committing.
Weekly and Daily Views
The Plan tab defaults to a weekly calendar view showing each day’s planned meals. Users can tap into a specific day for the daily detail view. The weekly view is the command center; the daily view is the execution view. Both views use consistent Radius.md cards on surfaceContainer backgrounds.
Auto-Generate My Week
For the user who doesn’t have time to plan: select the days you want to cook, tap “Auto-Generate,” and the system pulls contextually appropriate recipes to fill those slots. The user can then swap any meal with a single gesture or remove a meal entirely. Frictionless. No configuration panels. No settings.
Dinner Party Feature
This is a genuine hosting experience, not collaborative cooking:
Create a dinner party from a day in the Plan view. The dinner party icon appears muted (onSurfaceVariant) when no party exists, terracotta (primary) when one is planned.
Build a guest list. Invite friends via SMS (Twilio). Track RSVPs with clear status indicators.
Guests indicate dietary restrictions during RSVP. The chef sees consolidated dietary conflicts against the planned menu—flagged in warning token color with clear explanatory text.
Guests can see a live dinner status view during cook mode: what phase the meal is in, which courses are underway, estimated time to table. Read-only—the chef has exclusive controls.
Backend infrastructure is built: dinner_parties and dinner_guests tables, seven API routes, dinner-setup.tsx screen, web RSVP page.
5.4 — Grocery
Every recipe selection automatically populates the grocery list. Items are auto-categorized (Produce, Protein, Dairy, Pantry) via keyword matching. The user never manually enters categories.
In-Store Mode
When the user is at the grocery store, they check items off as they shop. Checked count badge updates in real time (success token fill). Clear completed and clear all actions available with destructive confirmation on clear all.
Pantry Inventory
A secondary tab within Grocery. Users track what they already have. The kitchen scanner (expo-camera) can add items to inventory. Over time, the system intelligently subtracts pantry items from the grocery list—you don’t need to buy olive oil if you already have a full bottle.
5.5 — Cook (The Flagship Experience)
Cook mode is the technical crown jewel. Already 1,800+ lines with premium features. This must be the most intuitive, most advanced guided cooking experience ever built in a mobile app.
Single-Recipe Cook Mode (Built)
Full-screen modal with expo-keep-awake. The screen never dims while cooking.
Step-by-step instructions with progress indicator, prev/next navigation, step counter.
Adaptive cooking language: three tiers of instruction voice, same ingredients.
Per-step ingredient matching—each step highlights exactly which ingredients you need.
Servings scaler—adjust servings, all quantities recalculate instantly.
Swipe gestures with haptic feedback for step navigation (spring animation, medium impact haptic).
Step timers with Reanimated transitions and success notification haptic on completion.
Liquid Glass Clear toolbar at bottom for navigation controls, keeping content area unobstructed.
Multi-Course Timeline Coordinator (In Progress)
The backward-planning timeline engine is Fork & Compass’s genuine competitive differentiator. No competitor offers this. The user sets a dinner time (7:00 PM). They’ve planned an appetizer, a main course, and a dessert. The system analyzes all prep times, cook times, and resting periods across every dish, then generates a unified timeline: “Dinner at 7 PM. Three dishes. Start at 4:25 PM. We’ll tell you what to do and when.”
Four Claude Code prompts are written and ready to execute in sequence:
Prompt 1: Types and Engine — types/kitchen.ts, utils/timelineEngine.ts
Prompt 2: Context and State — cooking context provider and state management
Prompt 3: Setup and Schedule screens — app/cooking-schedule.tsx
Prompt 4: Cook Mode integration, Handoff, and Finish flow
Solo cook mode is the focus. Two-person collaborative cooking is deferred to a later version to keep scope manageable and shippable.

11. Non-Negotiable Principles
These are the laws that govern every decision. When in doubt, return here.

Design is how it works. If a feature is beautiful but confusing, it has failed.
Subtract before you add. Every element must earn its place on screen.
Content is the hero. Food photography and cultural stories, not chrome and UI widgets.
Finish the back of the cabinet. The codebase, the admin dashboard, the API—invisible quality everywhere.
Follow the HIG. Tab bars at bottom. Back at top-left. Standard gestures. No exceptions.
Glass is for navigation, never for content. Liquid Glass effects frame the experience. Food photography stays crisp.
Speak like a human. Warm, active, culturally literate. Never robotic. Never blame the user.
Accessibility is architecture. VoiceOver, Dynamic Type, 48pt touch targets, reduced motion, color independence. Built in from day one.
One prompt, one concern. Claude Code prompts are focused, complete, and paste-ready. Commit after each.
Say no to protect the yes. Features are deferred, not deleted. But deferred means not built today.
